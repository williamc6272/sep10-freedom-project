Forensic science uses scientific and technological tools to investigate crimes and analyze evidence. Advances in technology such as DNA profiling, digital forensics, and improved imaging have made investigations faster and more accurate. As new innovations like AI and advanced genetics emerge, the future of forensic science promises even greater precision and new ways to solve complex crimes. Existing Forensic Science Technology

There are many existing technologies used in forensic science, each designed to support different parts of an investigation. Digital tools like bone identification databases and crime mapping software help investigators analyze evidence and recognize patterns more efficiently. In addition, device forensics technology allows experts to recover and store important data from electronic devices. Laboratory equipment, such as PCR preparation stations, is essential for DNA analysis because it helps prevent contamination and ensures accurate results. Systems like Next Generation Identification (NGI) also improve forensic work by enabling rapid identification through biometric data like fingerprints and iris scans. Together, these technologies show how modern forensic science depends on a wide range of tools to make investigations more accurate and effective, although their performance and capabilities can vary depending on their cost and level of advancement.

Prices of Existing Technology

The first website I looked into was OsetoId. This application is a morphological tool designed to help determine whether a bone is human or non-human and if non-human, to identify the animal species it belongs to. OsetoID is widely accessible and free to use across Windows, iOS, and other platforms. It is primarily utilized by anthropologists, medico-legal death investigators, crime scene personnel, coroners, medical examiners, and law enforcement professionals, providing an essential resource for accurate bone identification in forensic investigations.

During my first Google search for forensic science technology in the $500–$2,000 range, I looked into the NIJ-Funded Software Tools, Apps, and Databases. One of the tools featured on the website is a disk, a technological device used by modern forensic scientists. This disk helps scientists securely store important data, allowing valuable information to be preserved, shared, and remain actionable throughout investigations. Disks in this category typically range in cost from $500 to $2,000 and provide essential support for laboratories and crime scene personnel in managing and analyzing forensic data efficiently.

Research from the interface between forensic science and technology highlights tools that significantly enhance investigative accuracy. Fingerprint analysis devices are widely used to identify individuals through biometric data. PCR prep stations, such as the MY-PCR workstation, are compact laminar flow enclosures that create a personal clean zone for operators, preventing contamination of samples. These stations allow operators to amplify RNA and DNA reliably. Typically, PCR prep stations range in price from $500 to $5,000, function as thermocyclers, and use AC power. MY-PCR prep stations provide bench-top solutions that support forensic laboratories in producing accurate results while maintaining the integrity of biological samples.
Nano Tech Robots

One problem in the forensic world is the absense of evidence in a crime scence do to tamperment/destruction of the crime scene. In the future, microscopic forensic robots could be used to repair or reconstruct damaged evidence at a crime scene. These tiny machines would detect and follow specific frequencies or signal patterns, allowing them to locate broken particles of materials like fabric fibers, fingerprints, or DNA fragments. Reorganizing these particles into their original structure, the robots could help restore evidence that would otherwise be impossible to analyze. This technology would greatly improve accuracy in investigations and help solve cases that currently lack usable evidence. The microscopic forensic robots could be made from inexpensive materials like conductive components making each unit affordable to produce. They would also use low-energy systems, such as tiny rechargeable micro batteries or energy harvested from environmental frequencies, which reduces operating costs. This can help forensic scientists have a bigger budget to buy other stuff as well as being able to rapidly solve crime.
